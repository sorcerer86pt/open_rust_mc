Random-Ray FW-CADIS — head-to-head FOM measurement
====================================================

Three-way comparison: analog vs the older "lite" detector-backward
collision-density proxy (since removed) vs the random-ray multigroup
adjoint shipped this round, on the existing `shield_slab` photon-
transmission benchmark.

Setup:
- Source: 1 MeV monodirectional photon beam normal to z=0 face
- Geometry: water slab, reflective xy + z_back, vacuum z_front
- σ_t at 1 MeV ≈ 0.0707 cm⁻¹
- Source-side w_target normalised to 1.0; band width = ratio = 5.0
- Importance maps stored at 25–50 z-bins to match shield_slab's 1×1×n_z
  WeightWindow translator. Mesh resolution matters for FOM (see
  resolution sensitivity below).

300 cm (≈ 21 mfp) — beyond naive WW reach
====================================================

ratio=5  : T = 8.83e-9 ± 100% (1 transmitted in 1M)  — FOM = 0.020
ratio=50 : T = 0       (0 transmitted in 500k)       — FOM = 0.0

  Naive geometric splitting hits its limit at deep penetration: the
  required w_target ratio source-to-detector exceeds the band width
  × max_split combination, and photons either all roulette out near
  the source or never split enough to reach the detector. Source-
  distribution biasing + energy-dependent WW + larger max_split caps
  are the next-tier improvements. Out of scope for this round.

Bias check
====================================================

100 cm: RR CADIS T = 5.330e-3 vs analog T = 5.372e-3 → 0.5σ_combined.
        Unbiased.
200 cm: RR CADIS T = 1.252e-5 vs analog T = 1.105e-5 → 1.0σ_combined.
        Unbiased.

Resolution sensitivity
====================================================

A coarser n_z_bins reduces wall-time bloat from per-voxel splitting at
the cost of fidelity in the importance gradient. Sweep at 100 cm:

| n_z_bins | Wall (s, 200k) | σ_rel | FOM (/s) |
|---------:|---------------:|------:|---------:|
|       25 |            5.4 | 2.28% |    354   |
|       50 |           11.6 | 1.85% |    251   |

The 25–30 range is the practical sweet spot for slabs of this depth.
Finer meshes are warranted only when the importance gradient varies
on a sub-mfp scale.

Adaptive-ratio lever — negative result
====================================================

`WeightWindow::from_flux_adaptive` widens the band at low-importance
voxels (`ratio_v = base_ratio · (1 + ratio_growth · log10(φ_max/φ_v))`).
Wired into shield_slab as `--ww-growth`. Hypothesis: variable band
captures the depth-dependent optimal ratio (see fixed-ratio sweep
below), avoiding the per-thickness manual tuning.

Fixed-ratio sweep (motivation):

  100 cm:  ratio=2 → FOM=220, ratio=12 → FOM=317  (monotone-up)
  200 cm:  ratio=2 → FOM=0.7, ratio=3  → FOM=1.37, ratio=20 → FOM=0.77
                                                   (peaks at ratio≈3)

Adaptive sweep (1M histories @ 100cm, 2M @ 200cm):

| growth | FOM @ 100 cm | FOM @ 200 cm |
|-------:|-------------:|-------------:|
|    0.0 |       151.9  |        1.518 |  (= fixed ratio=5 baseline)
|    0.5 |       145.7  |          —   |
|    1.5 |          —   |        0.388 |

Conclusion: at converged statistics, adaptive ratio does **not** beat
fixed `ratio=5` on this benchmark. Wider source-side bands oversplit
in the transition region between source and detector. The lever is
implemented + unit-tested + exposed via `--ww-growth`, but the default
remains `0.0` (fixed ratio).

300 cm at any (ratio, growth) combination produces zero transmitted
photons in 500k histories — naive geometric splitting is bounded by
`max_split^n_voxel_crossings` and that exponential growth is capped
at `max_split = 8` per crossing. The required source-to-detector
w_target ratio at 21 mfp exceeds anything reachable. **The right
lever for >14 mfp is continuous-splitting along characteristics
(DXTRAN-style point-detector estimator) — research-tier work.**

Next-event estimator (v1) — biased, parked
====================================================

Implemented `--next-event` on shield_slab: at every Compton collision
the transport driver adds the deterministic forward-transmission
contribution `w × p_compton × ∫_0^1 KN_PDF(μ) · E'(μ) · exp(-Σ_t · (T-z)/μ) dμ`
to a separate `nee_tally` field on `HistoryResult`. Plus an uncollided
source-flight contribution at birth. Math module (`photon::nee`) is
unit-tested against analytic limits and the shared validated GL-16
quadrature (see `src/quadrature.rs`).

**Empirical result at converged stats (100 cm water, 1 MeV, 1M analog
vs 200k NEE):**

  Analog  : T = 5.292e-3 ± 1.12%  → FOM = 125
  NEE v1  : T = 6.542e-3 ± 1.19%  → FOM = 117

NEE estimate is **biased high by 23.6%**, a >13σ_combined deviation —
real bias, not statistical noise. Cause: the v1 NEE integrates the
free-electron Klein-Nishina angular DCS, but actual Compton scattering
in matter includes the bound-electron correction `S(x,Z)/Z`. At the
forward angles that dominate the integrand `(μ → 1, x → 0)`, the
bound correction `S/Z → 0`, so the free-KN forward DCS is much larger
than the actual bound DCS. NEE overcounts forward-scatter probability
and therefore over-estimates transmission.

The wall-time penalty of NEE is ~9× per history (16-point GL integral
at every collision). At 100 cm the reduced σ_rel doesn't compensate,
so FOM is roughly neutral against analog.

At 200 cm the analog reference is statistics-limited (1 transmitted in
100k histories at first measurement). NEE produces a self-consistent
σ_rel = 23%, T = 9.4e-6 — but the bias means the absolute T is
unreliable. Until the bound correction is added, NEE is parked as a
**v1 with a known accuracy bug**.

**Update (after debug round):**

Bound-electron correction was implemented (the v0 was free-KN-only):
the bound-corrected `compton_forward_transmission` now multiplies the
free-KN per-electron DCS by `Σ_e n_e · S(x, Z_e)` per quadrature node.
The integral matches the tabulated `incoherent_xs` macroscopic XS
within 5% (`integrated_bound_compton_dcs_matches_tabulated_macro_xs`
unit test). **The bias did not decrease** — went from +24% (free-KN)
to +21% (bound-corrected) at 100 cm. Bound correction is a real
physics improvement but isn't the dominant cause of the bias.

MCNP-style exclusion zone was also implemented per literature (the
classical fix for point-detector `1/r²` variance singularity):
collisions within `--nee-exclusion-cm R` of the detector face score
an analytically z-averaged contribution
`(μ/(Σ_t·R))·(1 − exp(−Σ_t·R/μ))` instead of the raw
`exp(−Σ_t·(T−z)/μ)`. Empirical sweep at 100 cm:

  R = 0    cm  → T = 6.40e-3, FOM = 119, bias +21%
  R = 1    cm  → T = 6.40e-3, FOM = 127, bias +21%
  R = 5    cm  → T = 6.46e-3, FOM = 269, bias +22%
  R = 14   cm  → T = 6.97e-3, FOM = 283, bias +32%
  R = 28   cm  → T = 9.08e-3, FOM = 436, bias +71%

The exclusion zone *worsens* the bias. The mechanism: for collisions
at z near T−R, the averaged factor is *larger* than the raw factor
(averaging over depths in [T−R, T] shifts the attenuation toward
shallower z where exp(−τ) is bigger). Summed over many collisions
inside the zone, this shifts the total tally upward — compounding the
per-collision-sum bias rather than cancelling it.

**The exclusion-zone literature is for *point detectors*, not planar
detectors.** For our planar slab face the integrand is bounded
(`(T−z)/μ → 0` is killed by `exp(−τ) → 1`, no `1/r²` pole), so the
classical regularisation doesn't apply.

**The actual bias source — diagnosed:**

Instrumented the NEE with a `--nee-trace` flag that decomposes the
tally per source photon into uncollided + first-collision + subsequent-
collisions contributions. At 100 cm / 1 MeV / 100 k histories:

  uncollided contribution = 8.56e-4   (matches analog uncollided ≈ 8.5e-4 ✓)
  first-collision NEE     = 1.20e-3
  subsequent collisions   = 4.42e-3   ← dominant
  total NEE               = 6.48e-3

Analog total = 5.30e-3 = uncollided 8.5e-4 + scattered 4.45e-3.

**The subsequent-collision summation (4.42e-3) is 3.7× the first-
collision contribution and is the bias driver.** First-collision-only
NEE plus uncollided ≈ 2.05e-3, well below analog total — that's
consistent with NEE being a single-Compton-direct-flight subset of
total analog. But once the per-collision summation across i > 1
fires, the running NEE total rapidly exceeds analog.

The theoretical argument that `Σ_i E[X_i × 1[reach i]]` is
*mutually-exclusive* (a photon escapes at most once, so the X_i
events partition realisations) appears to break in practice. The
most likely subtlety: my NEE_i = E[X_i | state i, channel sampled]
averages over hypothetical μ values, but the actual X_i depends on
the sampled μ. When subsequent collisions are reached (photon DIDN'T
escape forward at i), the sampled μ at i was non-forward — but my
NEE_i at i still added the full forward-hemisphere expectation. That
contribution is correctly counted at i, but then at i+1 we add ANOTHER
forward-hemisphere expectation conditional on the photon's new state
(which only arose because i was non-forward). The two contributions
are not strictly mutually exclusive; the conditional structure is
more subtle than the textbook proof admits in this slab geometry.

The fix is non-trivial. Either:
  1. Use the **track-length estimator** instead of the next-event
     estimator at collisions: tally `w · σ_compton · l · response(s)`
     along free-flight segments. This avoids the per-collision-sum
     issue entirely.
  2. Apply a "russian roulette + splitting" scheme on the NEE
     contributions to enforce that only ONE contribution per photon
     is counted (with appropriate weight adjustment).
  3. Switch to the surface-crossing estimator for the planar detector
     geometry — record `w · E_escape × μ_z / detector_area` for every
     photon that crosses the detector face, regardless of its scatter
     state.

Option 3 is the cleanest match for shield_slab — it's a textbook
unbiased planar-detector tally with no per-collision summation issue.
Option 1 (collision-flux estimator with track-length) is what
Serpent uses and is a drop-in replacement for the NEE math module's
output (just plug into a different transport hook).

For this round: the v1 NEE remains in tree behind `--next-event` with
clear documentation that it produces +21–38% biased-high estimates
and shouldn't be used for production transmission tallies.

The lever stays in tree behind `--next-event` and `--nee-exclusion-cm`,
both off by default. Math module + quadrature module + KN helpers
are all validated (302+ unit tests) and useful regardless. A future
session can take this on with fresh eyes.

Reading
====================================================

The 2026-05-07 round documented the lite proxy as **not delivering FOM
gain** — confirmed here at both depths (lite is 0.69× at 100 cm,
0.20× at 200 cm — actively worse than analog). The proxy peaks one
mfp back from the detector face (collision-density artefact) instead
of at the detector face (true importance peak), so the resulting WW
biases sampling away from the actual response region.

The random-ray multigroup adjoint flips the result at both depths:
2.2× FOM gain at 100 cm, 4.3× FOM gain at 200 cm. Both are unbiased
within combined MC σ. The lite path has been removed from the
codebase — the shipped substrate is `rr_cadis_slab` +
`random_ray::cadis::weight_window_from_adjoint` +
`random_ray::solver::RandomRaySolver`.

The textbook 50–1000× CADIS gain still isn't reached. That remains
gated on:

  1. Source-distribution biasing (sample initial position from the
     adjoint importance CDF instead of analog uniform).
  2. Energy-dependent WW (1 MeV is mostly Compton, but lower-energy
     photons after multiple scatters have different importance).
  3. Larger max_split + dynamic ratio at deep penetration.

These build on the random-ray adjoint substrate; none requires
revisiting the importance-proxy decision.

History
====================================================

The earlier "lite" detector-backward collision-density proxy was
removed from `shield_slab.rs` once the random-ray adjoint
demonstrated strict superiority on this benchmark. The previous
round's analysis (resume.md, 2026-05-07) documents what the lite
proxy did and why it fails; this file documents the empirical
measurement that justified replacing it.
