\section{GPU transport}
\label{sec:gpu}

The event-based CUDA solver~\cite{tramm2024} implements both
providers inside the same kernel; a runtime
\texttt{-{}-force-svd} flag clears the uploaded pointwise
tables so the SVD path reconstructs every reaction channel
from the basis.

\paragraph{GPU pointwise beats GPU SVD on PWR.}
On the large-L3 system GPU pointwise is $1.30\times$ faster than GPU SVD
($5\,967$ vs $7\,754$~ns/p, Table~\ref{tab:keff_pwr_desktop}).
The cause is specific to this geometry: four clad nuclides
(Zr-$90$, Zr-$91$, Zr-$92$, Zr-$94$) do not carry an MT$=4$
total-inelastic block in the ENDF/B-VII.1 HDF5; their total
inelastic is synthesised at lookup time by summing
$13$ discrete-level SVD kernels. On CPU the scalar pipeline
amortises this; on GPU it dominates the SVD-path runtime.

\paragraph{Per-warp $(n, E_\text{idx})$ cache: implemented, not enough.}
We implemented the per-warp shared-memory cache flagged in earlier
drafts as engineering work. The kernel keeps one slot per warp
($1+64$ doubles for level-sum and per-level XS, plus a $(\text{nuc},
E_\text{idx})$ key), populated by the warp's leader lane after the
energy-bin sort (\texttt{transport.cu::transport\_persistent}).
The slot caches the \emph{raw} \texttt{svd\_reconstruct} value per
level; the per-lane \mbox{$E\!\geq\!E_\text{thr}$} gate is applied
on read so that lanes straddling a level threshold (same
$E_\text{idx}$, opposite sides of the raw-eV threshold) inherit the
correct gating --- a subtlety that introduced a $+730$~pcm $k_\infty$
bias in our first attempt. With the corrected gating, a five-seed
$60$-batch $50\,000$-particle small-L3 run gives bit-parity within
SEM ($\Delta k_\infty = -18.5$~pcm vs cache-off, three-SEM gate
$208.8$~pcm) but a throughput ratio of only $1.003\times$
($23\,508 \to 23\,434$~ns/p). The gap to GPU pointwise is
\emph{not} closed; the cache is shipped enabled for
correctness preservation but is, for this geometry, a no-op on
performance.

\paragraph{Why the cache misses.}
The persistent kernel sorts particles into $256$ log-energy bins
spanning $\sim\!17$ decades, so a single bin covers a $\sim\!6\%$
energy range. After the sort, lanes within a warp share a bin but
not the per-nuclide $E_\text{idx}$, since the nuclide grids carry
$\mathrm{O}(10^4)$ points and a $6\%$ window straddles many of them.
The cache only activates when \emph{all} active lanes share the same
$(n, E_\text{idx})$, and the check fails on the great majority of
inelastic events in the energy-sorted PWR distribution. Closing the
gap likely requires either a finer pre-sort (e.g.\ on the per-nuclide
$E_\text{idx}$ directly), a per-thread shared cache (memory-budget
constrained at $\mathtt{launch\_bounds}(256, 2)$), or a different
representation of the discrete-level sum that does not require
$13$ reconstructions per inelastic event. As measured, GPU SVD on
PWR pin cell is \emph{not} a win over GPU pointwise.

\paragraph{Fidelity parity at large-L3 statistics.}
Post-correction GPU rows: GPU pointwise $k_\infty =
1.32821 \pm 0.00033$ and GPU SVD $k_\infty = 1.32762 \pm
0.00034$. The two GPU paths agree to $59$~pcm
($\approx 1.3\,\mathrm{SEM}_\text{combined}$), the two CPU
paths agree to $13$~pcm, and all four providers agree on
$k_\infty$ within $|\Delta_\text{OpenMC}| \le 51$~pcm with
the corrected stochastic-bin sampler. The GPU pointwise
row is the high point of the spread ($+51$~pcm vs OpenMC)
and remains within $3\,\mathrm{SEM}_{10}$; the other three
sit in the $-8$ to $-25$~pcm band.
